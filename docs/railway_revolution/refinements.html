Wooden rails severely lacked the integrity required to support such huge loads of coal and iron ore. It was not until Abraham Darby, a British engineer, developed a new method of smelting iron in the early 18th century that an economic solution arose. His process, called coking, was much more efficient than using charcoal-smelted iron and thus greatly increased the availability of iron. In the mid-1700's, miners began adding strips of iron to rails in an attempt to improve their durability.

As the iron industry improved further with Henry Cort's quality-increasing puddling process, ironmakers began making rails entirely of iron. These all-iron rails not only lasted much longer but also supported heavier burdens, laying the track for what could quite possibly be the most important development since the wheel1: steam power.


Great Britain led the world in iron production during the Industrial Revolution
