allegory: a work which can be interpreted on more than one level. [top]

alliteration: the repetition of consonant and vowel sounds at the beginning of words (e.g. "flip of a flipper"). [top]

allusion: a brief reference to a supposedly familiar place, event, or figure from literature, history, mythology, or the Bible. [top]

ambiguity: words or phrases which have more than a single meaning. [top]

apostrophe: figure of speech in which an inanimate object, abstract idea, or deceased individual is addressed as though it were alive (e.g. "rain, rain, go away"). [top]

assonance: the repetition of vowel sounds within closely positioned words (e.g. "green seaweed"). [top]

cacophony: the effect of using of harsh, discordant sounds. [top]

connotation: the implications of a word beyond its literal meaning. [top]

consonance: the repetition of consonant sounds within closely positioned words (e.g. "swerving and twisting, he swims"). [top]

euphony: a musical quality created by pleasant sounds and images. [top]

free verse: poetry which is not truly poetry in that it follows no regular rhyme or rhythm. [top]

imagery: the use of realistic description to stimulate the reader's senses. The seven types are visual (sight), auditory (hearing), tactile (feeling), olfactory (smell), gustatory (taste), kinesthetic (motion), and thermal (temperature). [top]

metaphor: an indirect comparison of two unlike objects (e.g. "her eyes are depthless oceans"). [top]

metre: a system of measuring the rhythms of a poem, according to stressed and unstressed syllables (see also scansion). [top]

metonymy: figure of speech in which an object is described by its function or parts (e.g. "the kettle is boiling" -- it is the water within which boils, not the kettle itself). [top]

mood: the dominant atmosphere of a literary work. [top]

onomatopoeia: figure of speech which involves a word whose sound suggests its meaning (e.g. "boom", "zip", "crash"). [top]

oxymoron: the deliberate placement of two contradictory words side by side for effect. [top]

paradox: a statement which at first appears to contradict itself but is in fact true. [top]

personifcation: the attribution of human qualities to something non-human such as animals, objects, or ideas (e.g. "death knocked at the door").

pun: a play on words, usually for humourous purposes (i.e. "comb on now, style my hair").

rhyme: the positioning of two similarly-sounding words together to emphasize this agreement of sound. Rhyme exists in two forms: end rhyme, where the last words on nearby lines rhyme, and internal rhyme, where the rhyme happens on the same line. The variation of rhyme where final consonants agree but the preceeding vowels do not is called slant or imperfect (i.e. might, fought). Another variation involves words which appears as though they rhyme but are pronounced differently (i.e. great, feat).

satire: the use of humour to ridicule and hopefully provoke change in society.

scansion: the process of analysing poetry to determine the metre and number of feet per line. Each combination of stressed and unstressed syllables has a special term (see table below). Also, there are terms describing the number of feet (syllables) per line, also shown below.

Syllables Term
unstressed, stressed iambic
stressed, unstressed trochaic
unstressed, unstressed, stressed anapestic
unstressed, stressed, unstressed amphibraic
stressed, unstressed, unstressed dactylic
stressed, stressed spondaic
unstressed, unstressed pyrrhic
Feet/line Term
one monometre
two dimetre
three trimetre
four tetrametre
five pentametre
six hexametre
seven heptametre
eight octametre

simile: a direct comparison of two unlike objects (i.e. her eyes are like the ocean).

stanza: a group of lines forming a unit in poetry (very similar to a paragraph). Stanzas can be classified accoring to the number of lines they contain, as shown in the table below:

Line number Term
two couplet
three tercet
four quatrain
six sestet
seven septet
eight octet

symbolism: the use of an object, person, or action which is significant beyond the apparent in an attempt to add complexity.

synecdoche: literary device in which the part represents the whole (e.g. "the crown rules our fair land" -- it is the king, not the crown).

theme: the often-hidden main idea or deeper meaning of a literary work.

tone: the attitude of the author towards the subject of a poem.
